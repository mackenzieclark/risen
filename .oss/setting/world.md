# Setting — Risen

## The Present Day
An ordinary contemporary city. Buses, group chats, late-night pharmacies, jobs people hate. The mundane world is rendered straight; it is the *baseline* against which the absurd is measured. The player begins fully inside it, at their lowest point.

## The Powers (Every Vampire, From Night One)
There is no slow climb to power. The newest fledgling is as grotesquely overpowered as a ten-thousand-year-old elder. From the first night a vampire can:
- **Compel** any human, instantly, effortlessly — total mind control.
- **Fly, teleport, and move as mist.**
- **Transform** into bats, wolves, vermin, fog — whatever.
- **Read minds, hypnotize, project thoughts** (telepathy and suggestion).
- Shrug off essentially any harm.

The crucial design fact: it is *immediately, blindingly obvious* to the newly-turned player that they could conquer the entire world with this, easily, and that no one else seems to have noticed or cared in millennia.

## The Society
A hidden, impossibly ancient, rigidly hierarchical secret order of predators — and a parody of itself.
- **Out of touch:** centuries or millennia behind the times; baffled by modernity; nostalgic for empires no one else remembers.
- **Obsessed with etiquette:** precedence, titles, seating, who hosts the Conclave, ancient feuds whose origins everyone has forgotten but no one will drop.
- **Profoundly serious about profoundly silly things.** Beings who could rewrite reality argue procedure.
- **Hierarchy is everything** — and yet a three-minutes-old fledgling is about to leapfrog all of it, because of the Prophecy.

Loose structure (to flesh out): Houses/Clans defined by ancient affectation rather than real difference; a governing **Conclave** of Elders; a moribund priestly order that keeps the Prophecy.

## The Prophecy of the Risen One
A long, threadbare messianic tradition: it is foretold that one night a **Risen One** will arise to unite the clans and rule the world "as was Written." There have been many false dawns; the society keeps believing anyway, because believing is tradition and tradition is identity. When the player is turned, the order decides — instantly, unanimously, on flimsy "evidence" endlessly re-cited — that it is *them*. The mechanism of selection is never explained. That mystery is deliberate; explaining it would kill the joke.
